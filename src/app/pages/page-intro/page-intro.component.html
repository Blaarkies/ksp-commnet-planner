Delta-v

Delta-v, dv, or formally Δv is a measure of change in velocity. This is the spaceship equivalent of talking about a car's range, or how much distance the car can travel without refueling. Because a spaceship in a stable orbit will continue travelling a huge distance without using any fuel at all, it is irrelevant measuring the spaceship's range in distance like we do with cars. Instead, spacecraft measure their capability in how much they can change their speed, as this ultimately determines if it can reach the Moon or beyond.

This delta-v can be measured practically by using up all the craft's fuel to thrust itself forward, and then finding the difference between the final velocity and the initial velocity. This difference, or ratherchange in velocity is the craft's delta-v capability. This is usually measured in meters per second m/s

Velocity is speed with a specific direction
The velocity of an object is the rate of change of its position with respect to a frame of reference.

Why is delta-v an important measure?

Generally, spacecraft use their fuel to speed up to reach higher orbits, and also use fuel to slow down when moving to a lower orbit. Both of these maneuvers primarily changed the craft's velocity, and knowing how much delta-v the spacecraft is capable of delivering will improves mission success.

Why spacecraft would speed up

Consider a craft in a stable low Earth orbit. Without using fuel, it will stay in such an orbit for a long time. If the craft were to thrust itself forward, using fuel to increase it's velocity, this would also increase the orbit's height. Thus if the craft continues to increase its velocity, its higher orbit could intersect with the Moon. The craft expended a specific amount of delta-v to reach this new velocity.


Why spacecraft would slow down

When a craft reaches the Moon, it will be approaching the Moon from outside of the Moon's gravitational influence. It will be pulled towards the Moon (it will "fall") and gain enough speed to swing by and escape the Moon's gravity. To reach a stable orbit, the craft has to thrust backwards to slow itself down. The craft would have to expend some delta-v to reach this new velocity.

Determining the total delta-v required for a mission

Moving around in space while taking advantage of orbital mechanics lends itself to a systematic set of steps to reach the destination. Similar to how a car can use a specific highway as part of many different trips, spacecraft also use similar maneuvers for different trips.

Let's look at the delta-v cost of the steps from the surface of Kerbin to land on Mun:

3400 m/s Launch from surface of Kerbin into Kerbin Low Orbit
852 m/s Burn prograde at Kerbin Low Orbit to reach an intercept with Mun
72 m/s Burn retrograde at Mun to slow down and capture into an elliptical orbit
199 m/s Burn retrograde to lower the elliptical orbit into a Mun Low Orbit
580 m/s Slow down at surface to land on Mun
5103 m/s Total delta-v required

Any efficient trajectory will move through some of these steps on its way to the destination. For example, a refueling mission to the Mun space station will go through all these steps, except it will not perform the Mun surface landing step. Alternative routes are ofcourse possible, but they will be less efficient and cost more delta-v to achieve the same result.

Commonly used terms
Burn; to execute a maneuver by using engine thrust
Prograde; the direction in which an object is moving
Retrograde; the opposite direction of which the object is moving

Calculating a craft's delta-v capability

A rocket is a vehicle that uses fuel to propel itself in order to deliver its payload to a destination. This thrust is ofcourse generated by rocket engines. It would seem quite difficult to determine the total delta-v capability if we look at the contributing factors:

  • Thrust is constant
  • Rocket mass decreases as fuel is used

Using delta-v efficiently

A craft's delta-v capability can be improved by adding more boosters, or reducing the payload. Alternatively, new players may find it easier by expending less delta-v to achieve the same results. There are many situations in space flight where the more efficient methods are counter-intuitive.

Launching into orbit from Kerbin

A craft on the launch pad requires these 2 conditions to be in a stable Kerbin orbit:
  • An altitude above the atmosphere (70 km for Kerbin)
  • A velocity towards the horizon (~2240 m/s)

As altitude cannot be directly manipulated, the rocket needs to achieve a vertical velocity of ~1172 m/s in order to reach a 70 km altitude. This implies that a rocket needs a ~2240 m/s horizontal velocity, and also a ~1172 m/s vertical velocity to reach orbit. It is inefficient to do these as 2 separate, sequential maneuvers. Instead, they should be executed as a gravity turn.

Mathematically adding these velocity vectors as a single maneuver shows how the single burn costs less delta-v for the same result, as opposed to doing a vertical burn, and then a horizontal burn. In addition to this, launching due East on Kerbin saves ~175 m/s due to Kerbin's rotation in that direction.

Gravity Turn
A launch maneuver performed at liftoff, where the rocket pitches over only slightly and then points prograde until it reaches orbit. The trajectory relies on gravity to "turn" the rocket towards the horizon, so that it reaches orbit when pointing at the horizon.

Launching into orbit from Mun

Launching into orbit from a planet/moon without an atmosphere does not require a gravity turn. The most efficient trajectory is to maximise horizontal velocity, while maintaining a minimum altitude to avoid collisions with mountains. The means that the rocket does not have to point prograde, but should instead pitch over aggressively at launch.

Landing on Mun

Landing on any planet/moon without at atmosphere follows the same rules as launching from them, but in reverse. To efficiently land, first move the craft into a very low orbit, then reduce speed until the trajectory intersects the intended landing location. Wait until the craft nears this point of impact, then perform a suicide burn. The craft pitch might need to be constantly adjusted above/below retrograde to ensure that the altitude reaches 0 m at the same time that the velocity reaches 0 m/s.

In practice there are more variables to consider (risk, craft TWR, mountains, landing target, etc.) which might require landing trajectories other than the near perfect shallow landing example described above.

Commonly used terms
Suicide burn; a maneuver where a landing craft fires up its engines at the last possible moment, as to arrest its velocity at the same time as touchdown
TWR; thrust-to-weight ratio, a measure of how many g's of acceleration a craft could sustain relative to a specific planet's gravity (Kerbin is commonly chosen for this, but the measure is different for every planet/moon)

Orbital maneuvers

Maneuvers in orbit are generally less expensive where the velocity is slowest. Massive changes in orbital inclination can be achieved at apoapsis for a lower cost. There are exceptions to this rule:

  • Burn at periapsis to change the apoapsis height, due to the Oberth effect
  • Radial changes to an elliptical orbit are often cheaper halfway between apoapsis and periapsis
  • Moving the orbit's inclination Ascending/Descending node forward/backward (as opposed to zeroing out the inclination) is often more effective at a point 90° ahead/behind of the node
Commonly used terms
Apoapsis; apex of arc, or the highest point in an orbit
Periapsis; the lowest point in an orbit
Oberth effect; phenomenon where a change in velocity produces a disproportionate change in kinetic energy

Interplanetary transfers

Players sending their first craft to Duna (or any other planet) are putting most of their skills and knowledge to the test. In addition, this is a great situation to learn more about delta-v efficiency. A common first mistake is to launch into a solar orbit with barely enough velocity to escape Kerbin's gravity, and then once in solar orbit, to perform another burn to match orbit with Duna and essentially rendezvous. This is extremely inefficient because of the high orbital velocities involved in solar orbits.

Efficient interplanetary transfer trajectories require that all maneuvers be executed at periapsis, which should ideally be as low as possible to extract a large advantage from the Oberth effect. The ejection burn should happen during a transfer window to minimize delta-v expenditure. This can seem immensely complex and high risk to new players, but even if performing this maneuver badly, it will still be less expensive than the solar-orbit-rendezvous method. It is expected that a small course correction maneuver be performed after the ejection burn, about halfway through the trip. This course correction can fine tune an intercept with Duna, and should not cost more than 20 m/s, but other planets with worse orbital inclinations could cost significantly more.

Commonly used terms
Ejection burn; maneuver that increases velocity to be faster than escape velocity
Escape velocity; minimum velocity required for a ballistic object to travel beyond the gravitational sphere of influence of the planet/moon it was orbiting
Transfer window; period of time during which a craft can cheaply perform an interplanetary transfer due to good alignment between the origin and destination planets/moons

Gravity assists and slingshots

When approaching a planet, a craft's speed measured relative to the planet, will be consistent between its entry and escape from the planet's gravity well as long as the craft remains ballistic (not actively changing course). This has the side effect that the craft's velocity changed direction after escaping the planet's gravity well. The craft's speed measured relative to the Sun could also change due to this, depending on how the planet curved the trajectory during the encounter.

Around Kerbin, this can be used to increase the craft's speed by simply crossing the orbit of Mun from behind. This will cause the craft's Mun relative velocity to be deflected towards the forward direction of Mun, adding Kerbin relative speed to the craft as it passes through the encounter. This can reduce the cost of reaching Minmus, since the final trajectory will have a lower Minmus relative velocity.

Missions to Jool can save upwards of 2000 m/s delta-v by using Tylo/Laythe to perform a reverse gravity assist. Trajectories that pass Tylo's orbit ahead of Tylo, will have their Tylo relative velocity redirected backwards, capturing the craft into a Jool orbit. A second gravity assist can lower the orbit to reach Laythe.

These techniques can be used for multiple chained gravity assists between planets to cheaply reach the destination, at the cost of longer transit durations. Eve and Kerbin are good planets to use as a springboard to reach farther targets. Planetary gravity assists are intimidating at first, but following some simple rules you can keep craft out of danger.

  • Orbits will always pass through the point of the last maneuver performed. If you only burn at the planet intercept location, you will always be able to still intercept the planet at some point in the future.
  • There is a practical limit to how much potential a gravity assist can provide, depending on the relative velocity and the planet's gravity. When the entry-escape trajectory deflects only a little, it is time to find an alternate gravity assist planet.

Refueling by docking to infrastructure

While not directly influencing the delta-v capability of a craft, refueling can have an tremendous effect on the total delta-v available on the mission. This does require some form of "infrastructure" along the mission trip (e.g. space station or surface base) for full effect, but some advantage can already be gained with a single launch vehicle.

Direct ascent versus rendezvous

When building a mission to land on Mun, it will often require less total mass (and less fuel) to build a 2-craft vehicle that undocks when in low orbit at Mun. The lander portion then performs the surface landing, while the command module portion stays in orbit, waiting for the lander to return to orbit and dock to transfer the crew. This requires less fuel to accomplish since the lander is not carrying the final portion of the mission down to the surface landing.

Space station in orbit

Launch mass can be reduced by designing the craft to only have enough delta-v to rendezvous with a Kerbin space station, and then refuel before starting the next leg of its trip (e.g. ~1123 m/s to reach a low Mun orbit). This allows the craft to be conservative with fuel during its journey, but it does come at the cost of refueling the space station eventually. This however can be done inexpensively due to economies of scale by using dedicated massive fuel tankers.

Fuel depots on the frontier

The price of fuel becomes more expensive the farther away the refueling location is, and this price difference is somewhat proportional to the delta-v needed to get there. When refueling a craft on Kerbin, this can be considered the cost price, but when refueling in Kerbin orbit we have to account for the initial launch costs for getting that fuel into orbit. This becomes worse for fuel in orbit around Duna, and even more for fuel at Moho. This can be avoided by investing in the ISRU parts to produce fuel on site, and this fuel can even be used as a discounted alternative to refuel other depots.


CommNet

CommNet or KerbNet refers to the control communication system in game. This is a limitation placed on the transmission of science, and the control of probe core craft that do not have crew onboard. Control can be regained as long as a connection can be made back to the KSC (or nearby craft that provide remote control)

The CommNet generally hinders players in early career mode from sending probes far out where much more science is available. Instead, their range slowly increases as the Tracking Station building is upgraded, or better communication antenna technology is unlocked. This range can however be increased earlier through the smart use of the relay satellites.

Communication satellite constellations

Placing a formation of relay satellites in the correct orbits can maximize the potential range of a signal back to Kerbin. These constellations should also consider how everything in orbit is always in motion; a rover on the far side of Mun needs a direct line-of-sight to Kerbin (or a relay satellite) in order to be controlled.

Ground based relays

Early launches can potentially run into signal issues as they launch eastward and lose line-of-sight to the KSC over the horizon, which then lack any control to do the final burn to reach a stable orbit. The fix for this involves placing a relay antenna due east which can provide a signal back to the KSC. Placing them on mountain tops helps to increase the range between them without the horizon being in the way.

Low orbit constellations

It is beneficial to have chain of relays close to Kerbin. These provide an important link to maintaining permanent communication with ground based vehicles, planes, other craft in low orbit, and especially craft that have re-entered the atmosphere waiting for you to activate their parachutes.

High orbit constellations

Placing 4 powerful relays in orbits higher than Mun can maintain a direct line-of-sight to its far side. This can help rovers stay in control before losing signal near a cliff. It is important that these relays be spaced equally, and maintain equal orbital periods as to not drift towards each other.

Primary planetary relays

A powerful relay in orbit around Duna will provide a connection to local craft for only a portion of its orbit. It loses signal when behind Duna, and thus will not provide signal to the local craft. This can be avoided by placing it in a highly elliptical polar orbit with the periapsis close to Duna's south pole. This will also pass behind Duna at some point in its orbit, but it will do so for a very short duration, and then move very slowly towards the apoapsis where it spends most of its time during the orbit. In over 99% of the orbital period, the relay will remain somewhere above the north pole with a direct line-of-sight to that hemisphere and most of the space surrounding Duna. This of course works for any other planet/moon as well.

Remote control stations

Crewed vehicles will always have some sort of control. A relay craft fitted with a RC-001S Remote Guidance Unit and a pilot on board, will also be able to provide remote control to probes in the local area. They will not be able to transmit science, but will have control to achieve landings and orbital maneuvers.
